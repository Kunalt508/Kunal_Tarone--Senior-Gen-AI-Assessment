Modern cloud applications adjust their compute footprint dynamically in
response to changing demand. Horizontal scaling adds or removes identical
service instances behind a load balancer, while vertical scaling resizes
the resources of a single instance. Reactive policies trigger on threshold
breaches such as sustained CPU above seventy percent, whereas predictive
policies use historical traffic patterns and machine learning forecasts
to provision capacity before demand arrives. Effective autoscaling depends
on fast instance startup, stateless service design, and warm pools that
keep pre-initialized capacity ready to absorb sudden bursts without
cold-start latency.

A load balancer distributes incoming network traffic across a pool of
backend instances to maximize throughput and minimize response time. Layer
4 balancers operate on TCP connections and route based on IP and port,
while Layer 7 balancers inspect HTTP headers, paths, and cookies to make
content-aware routing decisions. Common algorithms include round-robin
for uniform distribution, least-connections for unevenly-sized workloads,
and consistent hashing for session affinity. Health checks remove failing
instances from the rotation automatically, and weighted routing supports
gradual traffic shifts during deployments or canary releases.

Caching reduces latency and backend load by storing the results of
expensive operations closer to the consumer. Content delivery networks
cache static assets at edge locations geographically near end users,
in-memory caches such as Redis or Memcached hold frequently accessed
query results within microseconds of the application tier, and HTTP
caches respect directives like max-age and ETag for conditional requests.
The hard problem is invalidation: write-through, write-behind, and
time-to-live strategies each trade freshness against performance. A
well-tuned cache hierarchy can absorb the majority of read traffic and
dramatically reduce database pressure during traffic spikes.

When a single database instance cannot serve the required read or write
throughput, the data is partitioned across multiple nodes. Sharding
splits rows horizontally by a key such as user ID or geographic region
so that each shard owns a disjoint slice of the dataset. Replication
maintains copies of each shard across multiple nodes, providing fault
tolerance and the ability to serve reads from replicas. Replication can
be synchronous, where writes block until acknowledged by replicas, or
asynchronous, which is faster but allows replicas to lag. Consistency
models range from strong to eventual, each with different guarantees
about what a reader observes after a write.

When a downstream dependency becomes slow or unavailable, naive clients
retry aggressively and amplify the failure into a cascading outage. A
circuit breaker monitors the error rate of outgoing calls and trips open
after a threshold is exceeded, short-circuiting subsequent requests with
a fast failure instead of waiting for timeouts. After a cooldown the
breaker enters a half-open state and probes the dependency with a small
number of test requests before closing again. Combined with bounded
retries, exponential backoff with jitter, and per-call timeouts, this
pattern isolates failures and preserves overall system resilience even
when individual services degrade.

Observability is the ability to understand the internal state of a
running system from its external outputs. Metrics provide aggregated
numerical signals such as request rate, error rate, and latency
percentiles, sampled at regular intervals and stored in time-series
databases for alerting and dashboards. Structured logs record discrete
events with contextual fields useful for forensic investigation of
specific incidents. Distributed traces follow a single request across
service boundaries, recording the latency and outcome of each span so
engineers can pinpoint where time is being spent. Together these three
pillars allow operators to detect, diagnose, and resolve issues in
complex distributed deployments.

A message queue decouples producers from consumers by accepting work
items into a durable buffer that consumers process at their own pace.
This smooths bursty traffic, isolates failures so that a slow consumer
does not block the producer, and enables reliable retry of failed work
through dead-letter queues. Brokers such as Kafka, RabbitMQ, and SQS
offer different trade-offs in ordering guarantees, throughput, and
delivery semantics, ranging from at-most-once to exactly-once.
Asynchronous designs are essential for workloads like email dispatch,
video transcoding, and analytics ingestion, where the user does not need
to wait for completion.
